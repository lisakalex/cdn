Hotel “stars” are a way of grading the quality, service, and facilities a hotel offers — but the exact meaning depends on the country, because there’s no single worldwide standard.

Here’s a breakdown of what star ratings generally mean:

⭐ One-Star Hotel

  • Very basic, budget accommodation.

  • Small rooms, minimal furnishings.

  • Daily cleaning may not always be included.

  • Few or no on-site facilities.

⭐⭐ Two-Star Hotel

  • Basic but slightly more comfortable than one-star.

  • Private bathrooms usually included.

  • Limited amenities (like a small restaurant or bar).

⭐⭐⭐ Three-Star Hotel

  • Mid-range, comfortable accommodation.

  • Rooms usually larger with better furnishings.

  • On-site restaurant, room service, and reception open longer hours.

  • Fitness centre, business facilities, or pool may be available.

⭐⭐⭐⭐ Four-Star Hotel

  • High standard of comfort and service.

  • Spacious, well-furnished rooms.

  • Multiple dining options, 24-hour reception, concierge services.

  • Facilities such as spa, gym, conference rooms, and valet parking.

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Five-Star Hotel

  • Luxury, top-tier service and comfort.

  • Large, stylishly decorated rooms with premium bedding and amenities.

  • Personalised service: concierge, butler, chauffeur.

  • Fine dining, multiple high-end restaurants, wellness spa, swimming pools.

  • Strong focus on overall guest experience.

Who sets the standards?

  • UK: Hotels are rated by the AA (Automobile Association) and VisitEngland.

  • Europe: Many countries use the Hotelstars Union (used in Germany, Austria, Netherlands, etc.), which has specific criteria like room size, services, and amenities.

  • US: Ratings are done by private organisations like AAA (American Automobile Association) and Forbes Travel Guide.

  • International sites (like Booking.com, Expedia) often use their own internal ratings, which may differ from official ones.
